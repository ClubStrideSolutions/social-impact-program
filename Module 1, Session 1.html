Understanding Social Impact & YPAR 30 min

Discover how youth can drive meaningful change through participatory research:

  • What makes youth perspectives unique and valuable
  • Introduction to Youth Participatory Action Research
  • Examples of youth-led community transformation
+

Key Terms

Social Impact: Creating positive, meaningful change in communities through intentional action.

Youth Participatory Action Research (YPAR): A research approach where young people investigate issues affecting their lives and take action based on findings.

Co-researcher: Youth working as equal partners with adults in the research process.

Community Assets: Existing strengths and resources within a community that can be leveraged for positive change.

Discussion Points

  • Why are youth uniquely positioned to identify and address community challenges?
  • How does YPAR differ from traditional research where youth are just subjects?
  • What community changes have you witnessed that were led by young people?
  • How can research become a tool for advocacy and action?

Activity: Youth Power Mapping

In small groups of 3-4:

  1. Share one example of when you or other youth made a positive change (big or small)
  2. Identify what made that change possible (skills, resources, support)
  3. Create a visual "power map" showing youth assets in your community
  4. Present your map to the larger group, highlighting untapped potential

Time: 15 minutes for mapping, 15 minutes for sharing

Reflection Questions

  • What youth assets surprised you during the mapping exercise?
  • How might YPAR help amplify youth voice in your community?
  • What excites you most about becoming a co-researcher?
  • What concerns or questions do you have about the research process?

Public Health & Social Determinants 30 min

Explore the factors that shape community health beyond healthcare:

  • Understanding Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)
  • Connecting individual health to community conditions
  • Identifying local SDOH in action
+

Key Terms

Public Health: The science of protecting and improving the health of communities through education, policy, and research.

Social Determinants of Health (SDOH): The conditions where people are born, grow, work, live, and age that affect health outcomes.

Health Equity: When everyone has a fair opportunity to attain their full health potential.

Upstream Factors: Root causes that create health problems before they manifest as illness.

Discussion Points

  • How do factors like housing, education, and transportation affect health?
  • Why might two neighborhoods in the same city have different health outcomes?
  • What SDOH do you see impacting youth in your community?
  • How can addressing SDOH create more lasting change than treating symptoms?

Activity: SDOH Detective

Using a provided community scenario:

  1. Read about "Maria," a local high school student facing multiple challenges
  2. Work in pairs to identify all the SDOH affecting her situation
  3. Create a web diagram showing how these factors connect and reinforce each other
  4. Propose 2-3 "upstream" interventions that could improve multiple outcomes

Example SDOH to consider: Food access, transportation, safe housing, education quality, family income, neighborhood safety

Reflection Questions

  • Which SDOH connections surprised you the most?
  • How does understanding SDOH change your view of health problems?
  • What SDOH most affects youth in your own community?
  • How might youth-led research address these root causes?

Community Mapping & Team Building 30 min

Collaborate to identify and visualize community needs:

  • Collective brainstorming of local challenges
  • Creating visual community maps
  • Building team connections and trust
+

Key Terms

Community Mapping: A participatory process of identifying and visualizing community assets, challenges, and relationships.

Stakeholder: Any person or group with interest in or influence over community issues.

Systems Thinking: Understanding how different parts of a community interact and influence each other.

Collective Intelligence: The enhanced problem-solving that emerges from group collaboration.

Discussion Points

  • What makes a community challenge worth researching?
  • How do different perspectives enhance our understanding of issues?
  • Why is trust important in youth research teams?
  • How can mapping help us see connections we might miss individually?

Activity: Collaborative Community Challenge Map

As a full group with large paper or digital whiteboard:

  1. Silent Brainstorm (5 min): Everyone writes challenges they see on sticky notes
  2. Clustering (10 min): Group similar challenges together into themes
  3. Connection Drawing (10 min): Draw lines between related issues
  4. Priority Voting (5 min): Each person gets 3 dots to vote on most pressing issues

Team Building Element: Share your name and one strength you bring to addressing these challenges

Reflection Questions

  • What patterns emerged from our collective mapping?
  • Which connections between issues surprised you?
  • How did hearing others' perspectives change your understanding?
  • What challenge area interests you most for potential research?

Session Assignment

Complete this reflective assignment to deepen your understanding and prepare for Session 2.

Community Observation Journal Due Next Session

  • Observe your community through a "researcher's lens"
  • Document examples of SDOH in action
  • Reflect on potential research questions
✓

Instructions:

  1. Observation Walk (20 minutes): Take a walk through your neighborhood or community space. Notice things with fresh eyes - what do you see that affects health and wellbeing?
  2. Document 3 SDOH Examples: Write about three specific examples of Social Determinants of Health you observe (e.g., bus stop conditions, food store options, youth gathering spaces)
  3. Reflection Questions (1-2 paragraphs):
    • What surprised you during your observation?
    • How might these conditions affect youth specifically?
    • What questions emerged that could guide future research?
  4. Initial Research Question: Based on your observations, write one potential research question you'd like to explore using YPAR methods

Format: 1-2 pages typed or neatly handwritten. Include the date, time, and location of your observation walk.

Purpose: This assignment helps you practice observation skills and connect classroom concepts to real community conditions.
