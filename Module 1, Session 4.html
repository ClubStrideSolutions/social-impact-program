KPI Development for Public Health 30 min

Create meaningful metrics for youth health initiatives:

  • Understanding different types of indicators
  • Balancing process and outcome measures
  • Building youth-friendly dashboards
+

Key Terms

Key Performance Indicator (KPI): A quantifiable measure used to evaluate the success of an initiative in meeting objectives.

Process Measure: Indicators that track activities or steps taken (e.g., number of workshops conducted).

Outcome Measure: Indicators that measure the results or impact (e.g., improvement in health behaviors).

Leading Indicator: Metrics that predict future performance or outcomes.

Lagging Indicator: Metrics that show what has already been accomplished.

Dashboard: A visual display of KPIs that provides at-a-glance monitoring of progress.

Discussion Points

  • What makes a KPI meaningful versus just easy to measure?
  • How do we balance quantitative data with qualitative stories?
  • Why might process measures be as important as outcome measures?
  • How can KPIs help secure continued funding and support?

Activity: Building Your KPI Framework

Part 1 - KPI Categories (10 mins): Review examples from youth health programs:

  • Reach: How many youth are we connecting with?
  • Engagement: How actively are they participating?
  • Knowledge: What are they learning?
  • Behavior: What actions are they taking?
  • Impact: What community changes result?

Part 2 - Design Your KPIs (15 mins): Using your SMART goal from Session 3:

  1. Create 2 process KPIs (what you'll do)
  2. Create 2 outcome KPIs (what will change)
  3. For each KPI, specify:
    • What exactly will be measured
    • How data will be collected
    • How often it will be measured
    • What success looks like

Part 3 - Dashboard Sketch (5 mins): Draw a simple dashboard showing how you'd display these KPIs visually for easy tracking.

Reflection Questions

  • Which KPIs will be most meaningful to youth participants?
  • How might your KPIs need to adapt over time?
  • What challenges do you anticipate in data collection?
  • How will you ensure KPIs don't become more important than actual impact?

Feedback Loops for Continuous Improvement 30 min

Design systems that learn and adapt:

  • Understanding reinforcing and balancing loops
  • Creating community feedback mechanisms
  • Building adaptive programs
+

Key Terms

Feedback Loop: A system where outputs are routed back as inputs, creating continuous improvement.

Reinforcing Loop: A cycle that amplifies or accelerates change (positive or negative).

Balancing Loop: A cycle that seeks equilibrium and prevents runaway change.

Adaptive Management: Adjusting strategies based on ongoing learning and feedback.

Iteration: Repeated cycles of implementation, feedback, and improvement.

Discussion Points

  • How do existing problems often create reinforcing loops that make them worse?
  • What role can youth play in breaking negative cycles?
  • How quickly should programs adapt based on feedback?
  • What happens when we ignore community feedback?

Activity: Mapping Feedback Systems

Part 1 - Identify Current Loops (10 mins): In pairs, map a reinforcing loop in youth health:

Example: Low energy → Poor school performance → Stress → Poor sleep → Low energy

  • Draw your loop with arrows showing connections
  • Identify where youth interventions could break the cycle

Part 2 - Design Feedback System (15 mins): For your initiative, create:

  1. Input Methods: How will you gather feedback?
    • Youth surveys
    • Community forums
    • Digital feedback
    • Observation data
  2. Analysis Process: Who reviews feedback and how often?
  3. Response Timeline: How quickly will you make changes?
  4. Communication Loop: How will you show the community their feedback created change?

Part 3 - Share Solutions (5 mins): Present one innovative feedback mechanism to the group.

Reflection Questions

  • How can feedback loops empower youth rather than just evaluate them?
  • What might prevent honest feedback in your community?
  • How do you balance stability with responsiveness to feedback?
  • What feedback are you most eager (or afraid) to receive?

Framework Integration & Action Planning 30 min

Synthesize all learning into a comprehensive approach:

  • Connecting YPAR, ethics, analysis, and measurement
  • Creating your integrated action plan
  • Preparing for Module 2
+

Key Terms

Framework Integration: Combining multiple approaches into a cohesive strategy.

Systems Approach: Viewing initiatives as part of larger interconnected systems.

Strategic Alignment: Ensuring all activities support overarching goals.

Sustainability Plan: Strategies for maintaining impact beyond initial implementation.

Discussion Points

  • How do all four frameworks (YPAR, Ethics, 5 Whys/SMART, KPIs/Feedback) strengthen each other?
  • What happens when we skip one of these elements?
  • How can youth researchers maintain this comprehensive approach under pressure?
  • What support systems help sustain integrated approaches?

Activity: Your Integrated Action Plan

Individual/Small Group Work (20 mins): Create a one-page visual plan that includes:

1. YPAR Foundation (Session 1):

  • Community issue you're addressing
  • Youth role as co-researchers

2. Ethical Framework (Session 2):

  • How you'll protect participants
  • Cultural considerations

3. Root Cause & Goals (Session 3):

  • Core root cause identified
  • One SMART goal

4. Measurement & Adaptation (Session 4):

  • 2-3 key KPIs
  • Feedback loop design

5. Next Steps:

  • First 3 actions to take
  • Resources needed
  • Timeline for Module 2

Gallery Walk & Feedback (10 mins): Post plans and provide peer feedback using "I like... I wonder... I suggest..." format.

Reflection Questions

  • How has your understanding of community change evolved over these four sessions?
  • Which framework feels most natural? Most challenging?
  • What excites you most about moving into Module 2?
  • How will you maintain this integrated approach when facing real-world pressures?

Module 1 Culminating Assignment

Demonstrate your readiness for Module 2 by creating a comprehensive research proposal.

Comprehensive Framework Application Due Before Module 2

  • Synthesize all four sessions' learning
  • Create a preliminary research proposal
  • Prepare to pitch your idea
✓

Instructions: Create a 3-5 page research proposal that includes:

  1. Executive Summary (1/2 page):
    • Brief overview of your community issue and proposed solution
    • Why youth leadership is essential for this issue
  2. Background & Root Cause Analysis (1 page):
    • Community context and SDOH factors
    • Complete 5 Whys analysis
    • Evidence supporting your root cause identification
  3. Research Plan & Ethics (1 page):
    • YPAR methodology you'll use
    • Ethical considerations and consent process
    • How you'll ensure cultural sensitivity
    • Data protection plans
  4. Goals & Implementation (1 page):
    • 2-3 SMART goals with clear connections to root causes
    • Timeline for implementation
    • Resources needed
    • Potential partners
  5. Measurement & Sustainability (1 page):
    • KPI framework with 4-6 indicators
    • Feedback loop design
    • How the project will continue beyond initial implementation
    • Youth leadership succession plan
  6. Visual Elements (included in page count):
    • Systems map showing problem connections
    • Simple logic model or theory of change
    • KPI dashboard mockup

Presentation: Prepare a 5-minute pitch of your proposal for the first day of Module 2. Focus on problem significance, your innovative approach, and expected impact.

Purpose: This proposal demonstrates your mastery of Module 1 concepts and prepares you to hit the ground running with actual research in Module 2.
