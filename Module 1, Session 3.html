The 5 Whys Framework 30 min

Discover the power of asking "why" to uncover root causes:

  • Introduction to root cause analysis
  • Simple 5 Whys demonstration
  • Guided practice with youth issues
+

Key Terms

Root Cause Analysis: A method that identifies the underlying source of a problem rather than treating symptoms.

5 Whys Technique: A simple technique of asking "why" multiple times (usually 5) to dig deeper into an issue.

Surface Symptom: The visible problem that is actually caused by deeper issues.

Causal Chain: The sequence of causes and effects that lead to a problem.

Discussion Points

  • Why do we often address symptoms instead of underlying causes?
  • How can asking "why" help us understand youth health issues better?
  • What happens when we stop at the first or second "why"?
  • How might different people arrive at different root causes for the same problem?

Activity: 5 Whys in Action

Part 1 - Demo (10 mins): Follow along as we demonstrate the 5 Whys with a real example:

  • Problem: "Students aren't accessing free mental health services at school."
  • Why? "They don't know about them."
  • Why? "Information isn't reaching students effectively."
  • Why? "Announcements use formal language and adult-focused channels."
  • Why? "Student input wasn't included in communication planning."
  • Why? "There's no system for youth participation in service design."

Part 2 - Practice (15 mins): In pairs, choose ONE problem and apply 5 Whys:

  1. Youth skipping breakfast
  2. Low attendance at after-school programs
  3. Students not completing homework
  4. Teens avoiding physical activity

Part 3 - Share (5 mins): Share your root cause with another pair. Did you arrive at similar or different conclusions?

Reflection Questions

  • What surprised you about the root causes you identified?
  • How deep did you need to go before finding a meaningful root cause?
  • How might this technique change your approach to community problems?
  • When might 5 Whys not be enough (or be too many)?

SMART Goal Framework 30 min

Transform insights into actionable objectives:

  • Understanding SMART components
  • Converting vague ideas to clear goals
  • Practice with community issues
+

Key Terms

SMART Goals: Goals that are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound.

Specific: Clearly defines what will be accomplished and by whom.

Measurable: Includes concrete criteria for tracking progress and success.

Achievable: Realistic given available resources and constraints.

Relevant: Aligned with broader objectives and community needs.

Time-bound: Has a clear deadline or timeframe.

Discussion Points

  • Why do vague goals often fail to create change?
  • How can specific metrics help us track progress?
  • What makes a goal achievable versus unrealistic?
  • How do we ensure our goals address real community needs?

Activity: SMART Goal Transformation

Part 1 - Analysis (10 mins): Review these examples together:

  • Vague: "Increase youth awareness about mental health."
  • SMART: "By May 30, 2025, deliver mental health workshops to 200 students across 4 high schools, with 80% reporting increased knowledge of coping strategies."

Part 2 - Transformation (15 mins): In small groups, transform these vague goals:

  1. "Help students eat healthier."
  2. "Reduce youth stress."
  3. "Improve community safety."
  4. "Increase youth voice in decisions."

For each, identify all 5 SMART components clearly.

Part 3 - Peer Review (5 mins): Exchange goals with another group. Are all SMART elements present? Provide constructive feedback.

Reflection Questions

  • Which SMART component was hardest to define? Why?
  • How does making goals measurable change your approach?
  • What resources would you need to achieve your SMART goals?
  • How might SMART goals help in securing support for your projects?

Integration: Root Cause to Action 30 min

Connect analysis to action planning:

  • Linking root causes to SMART goals
  • Developing action steps
  • Creating your first action plan
+

Key Terms

Theory of Change: How your actions will lead to desired outcomes based on root cause analysis.

Action Steps: Specific tasks needed to achieve your SMART goal.

Logic Model: Visual representation connecting root causes, interventions, and expected outcomes.

Systems Intervention: Actions that address root causes rather than symptoms.

Discussion Points

  • How does understanding root causes change our intervention strategies?
  • Why is it important to connect every goal back to a root cause?
  • How can we ensure our actions create lasting change?
  • What happens when we skip from problem identification straight to solutions?

Activity: Your First Action Plan

Individual/Pair Work (20 mins): Create a mini action plan:

  1. Select a Problem: Choose from your earlier 5 Whys analysis or a new issue
  2. Apply 5 Whys: Identify at least one significant root cause
  3. Create SMART Goal: Develop one goal that addresses this root cause
  4. List Action Steps: Write 3-5 specific actions needed to achieve your goal
  5. Identify Resources: What support, skills, or materials do you need?
  6. Set Timeline: When will each action step happen?

Gallery Walk (10 mins): Post your action plans around the room. Walk around, read others' plans, and leave sticky note feedback with:

  • One strength of their plan
  • One suggestion or resource to offer

Reflection Questions

  • How did root cause analysis influence your goal setting?
  • What patterns did you notice across different action plans?
  • Which action steps feel most achievable? Most challenging?
  • How will you know if your intervention addresses the root cause?

Session Assignment

Apply these frameworks to a real community issue you care about.

Community Problem Analysis & Goal Setting Due Next Session

  • Apply 5 Whys to a community issue
  • Develop a SMART goal addressing the root cause
  • Create an action plan outline
✓

Instructions:

  1. Choose Your Issue:
    • Select a youth/community issue you've observed (can build on Session 1 reflection)
    • Briefly describe why this issue matters to you personally
  2. 5 Whys Analysis (1 page):
    • State the problem clearly
    • Document each "why" question and answer
    • Continue until you reach a meaningful root cause
    • Explain why you believe this is the root cause
  3. SMART Goal Development:
    • Write one SMART goal that addresses your root cause
    • Label each component (S-M-A-R-T) clearly
    • Explain how this goal connects to the root cause
  4. Action Plan Outline:
    • List 5-7 specific action steps
    • Identify who needs to be involved
    • Note potential challenges and solutions
    • Create a simple timeline
  5. Reflection (1 paragraph):
    • How did these tools change your understanding of the problem?
    • What excites or concerns you about implementing this plan?

Format: 2-3 pages total, typed or neatly handwritten. Include any diagrams or visuals that help explain your thinking.

Purpose: This assignment integrates all of today's learning and prepares you to begin developing real solutions to community challenges.
